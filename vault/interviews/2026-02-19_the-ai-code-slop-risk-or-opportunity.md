## Summary
Sarah Guo and Elad Gil dedicate this episode of No Priors to dissecting the "SaaS-pocalypse" narrative that has gripped tech markets. While acknowledging the long-term transformational power of AI, they argue that the short-term panic over the death of SaaS is dramatically overstated. The idea that Fortune 100 companies will replace their enterprise software with vibe-coded internal tools ignores the realities of enterprise sales, change management, security, and organizational complexity. They note that even AI-native portfolio companies with hundreds of millions in revenue are rapidly hiring salespeople, not replacing them with "vibe sales."

At the same time, they highlight signals the market is underappreciating. AI labs are achieving the fastest revenue ramp in software history, going from $1B to $10B in roughly a year -- a journey that took ADP and Adobe over 20 years. Token pricing is collapsing at staggering rates: GPT-4 equivalent models dropped 150x in cost over 21 months, and O1 equivalents dropped 88x in just 11 months. Meanwhile, inference clouds are growing consumption by 1000x. Technology now represents about 12% of US GDP (up from 4% in 2005), and could reach 15-30% by 2035 as AI converts service-sector work into software spend.

The hosts draw important parallels to the internet era rather than the more stable SaaS/cloud era. Just as Friendster gave way to Facebook and Lotus 1-2-3 was displaced by Excel, the current period of rapid technological change means that seemingly dominant companies can be displaced in years rather than decades. They note that AI labs will inevitably forward-integrate into the most important vertical applications -- a pattern seen with every major platform shift from Microsoft to Google. This creates real risk for startups building single-point products on top of AI platforms.

For founders, the actionable advice is twofold: build multi-product bundles for defensibility rather than relying on the SaaS-era "do one thing well" approach, and pre-schedule non-emotional board discussions about exit timing. Most companies have roughly a 12-month window of peak value, and the compressed cycle times of AI (where "every two years is ten years") make it critical to be intellectually honest about competitive position and timing. The discussion frames the current moment as simultaneously revolutionary and overhyped -- the technology is genuinely transformational, but the market is extrapolating too aggressively from demos and small-team behavior to enterprise reality.
