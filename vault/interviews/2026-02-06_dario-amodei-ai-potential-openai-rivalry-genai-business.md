## Summary
In this wide-ranging 68-minute interview at Anthropic headquarters in San Francisco, CEO Dario Amodei makes a forceful case that AI is on an exponential trajectory that most people are underestimating. Drawing an analogy to the Internet boom of the 1990s, he argues that two years before an exponential "goes crazy," it looks like it is just getting started. He backs this up with Anthropic's own revenue numbers: approximately 10x growth each year, from zero to $100M in 2023, to $1B in 2024, and above $4.5B by mid-2025, which he says makes Anthropic the fastest-growing software company in history at its scale.

Amodei pushes back against claims of diminishing returns from scaling, citing SWE-Bench scores growing from 3% to 72-80% across recent Claude model generations. He argues that even without solving continual learning -- a critique raised by Dworkesh Patel -- the economic impact of current LLMs would be enormous, comparing it to having 10 million Nobel Prize winners who cannot read new textbooks but can still make breakthroughs. He believes context windows expanding to 100 million words and new reinforcement learning approaches will address remaining gaps.

On the business side, Amodei explains Anthropic's bet on enterprise and business AI use cases over consumer applications, arguing this creates better incentives for model improvement. He addresses concerns about profitability by explaining that while the company appears to lose money ($3B estimated), each individual model generation is profitable -- the apparent losses come from investing in training the next, more expensive model. He is dismissive of open source AI as a competitive threat, calling it a "red herring," and expresses strong skepticism about Meta's talent acquisition strategy, saying "they are trying to buy something that cannot be bought."

The interview takes a personal turn as Amodei discusses his father's death from a disease that became curable just 3-4 years later (cure rate went from 50% to 95%), which drove him from theoretical physics to biology and eventually to AI. He views AI as the only technology capable of bridging the gap in biological complexity that individual human researchers cannot. This personal motivation underpins his passionate rejection of the "doomer" label: he argues that accelerationists lack a genuine humanistic understanding of AI's benefits, while pure doomers make "gobbledygook" arguments that safety is logically impossible. His approach is a "multi-step game" of investing in safety technology while continuing to develop capabilities, publicly advocating for export controls and responsible scaling policies even when it draws criticism from peers running trillion-dollar companies.
