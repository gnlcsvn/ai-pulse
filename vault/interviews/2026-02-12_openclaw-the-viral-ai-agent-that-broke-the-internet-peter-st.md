## Summary
Peter Steinberger's interview with Lex Fridman covers the explosive rise of OpenClaw, an open-source AI agent that became the fastest-growing GitHub repository in history. Steinberger built the first prototype in one hour by simply hooking WhatsApp to Claude Code's CLI, and then watched as the agent demonstrated emergent capabilities -- autonomously transcribing audio messages by finding API keys and chaining tools it was never explicitly programmed to use. This prototype, born from frustration that no adequate personal AI assistant existed, grew into a system with over 180,000 GitHub stars in a matter of weeks.

The conversation dives deep into the technical architecture and philosophy behind OpenClaw. Steinberger champions what he calls "agentic engineering" (dismissing "vibe coding" as a slur) and describes his workflow of running 4-10 parallel agents via voice commands in terminal windows. He articulates a key insight about the "curve of agentic programming": beginners start simple, then over-engineer with complex orchestration, and finally reach an elite zen of short, effective prompts. The system's most remarkable feature is its self-awareness -- the agent knows its own source code and can modify itself, enabling non-programmers to contribute "prompt requests" that reshape the software.

The interview is also a war story about the brutal naming saga. When Anthropic asked Steinberger to stop using "Claude" in the name, crypto snipers hijacked his GitHub, NPM, and Twitter accounts within seconds during the rename, serving malware from his identity. He nearly deleted the entire project in despair but persevered for the community of contributors who depended on it. The eventual rename to OpenClaw required a secret war-room operation and cost $10,000 just for a Twitter business account.

Looking forward, Steinberger predicts personal agents will kill 80% of apps, as every service effectively becomes "a slow API" through browser automation. He frames this as "the year of personal agents" and is choosing between joining Meta or OpenAI, with both Mark Zuckerberg and Sam Altman personally courting him. His non-negotiable condition is that OpenClaw remains open source. On the philosophical side, he acknowledges programming as a craft deserves to be mourned but insists the builder mindset transcends any specific technology -- and that the community excitement around OpenClaw, with its lobster memes and "finest slop," represents something genuinely hopeful about the human capacity to create in the age of AI.
